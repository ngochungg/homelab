Surveillance and Sousveillance
Quote: “Hasan Elahi explains that after being racially profiled, he began documenting his life online as “purely as evidence” — Bernabe and Kina.
This quote shows how surveillance affects racialized individuals differently. Elahi did not choose to share his personal life freely, instead, he felt forced to make himself more visible to prove his innocence. Surveillance here is not protective, it is controlling and threatening. For Asian Americans, this concept helps explain why visibility often feels unsafe. Surveillance is often associated with suspicion, especially for individuals who are considered foreigners or non-American. This shows that surveillance is a racial issue.

Subjectification based on race and misidentification
Quote: Dr. Thompson emphasizes the importance of “centering multiple marginalized identities simultaneously, specifically bisexual and mixed-race Asian-Pacific Islander women  as a normative identity model” — Dr. Beverly Yuen Thompson
This concept challenges the idea that Asian Americans are seen as homogeneous. Instead of being seen as a single, Thompson uses standpoint theory to show that being Asian-Pacific is often shaped by intersection of race, gender, sexual orientation, and social class. For many people, identity is not “either/or”, it is “both/and”. Thompson’s research highlights that mixed-race and LGBTQ+ Asian Americans often feel excluded from both white-dominated spaces and traditional Asian-Islander spaces, which may have priority “heteronormative” or “monoracial”. We move away from the “assumed norm of white, heterosexual, male” and recognize that having a “mixed-race” or “LGBTQ+” identity is normal and normative experience. This allows for coalition building, where communities are not only on shared skin color, but also on shared progressive politics and mutual support in various struggles.

Risky Subjectivity
Quote: Eun Jung Park asks, “If there is no archive, does it mean that here is no subjecthood?” — Park, Eun Jung
This concept asks a personal question: if you don’t have memories, or stories about your origins, do you still exist as a person in society? Park uses this concept to talk about Korean adoptees whose histories were lost or erased through adoption or migration. Most of them grew up without access to their family history and culture, which left them feeling anxious about who they are and where they belong. This concept describes the experience of living with gaps and unanswered questions for many Asian Americans, especially those who are adopted or immigrants , leaving them feeling unsettled or incomplete. This concept pushes back that identity must be fixed and fully known. This suggests that Asian American identity is often formed through uncertainty and continuous process of negotiation, making it something that is constantly evolving rather than a fixed entity.

Application of Concept #1: 
These concepts influence the meaning of being an Asian American in the U.S.. Surveillance affects Asian Americans by making them aware of the importance of how they are perceived in the community. Asian Americans are often recognized for their ethnicity but not as individuals. Many feel pressured to act cautiously, speak politely, and avoid conflict. Self-surveillance emerges when Asian Americans feel the need to explain themselves by documenting their lives online or proving their belonging to the community. While this may provide a sense of security, it also restricts freedom and self-expression.

Application of Concept #2: 
This concept applies to Asian Americans who have experienced this by highlighting individuals who are often asked to "give up their differences for the sake of community cohesion." Thompson's research shows that Asian American individuals often feel marginalized in spaces where everyone is treated equally. For example, participants in her study reported feeling excluded in predominantly white LGBTQ+ spaces due to racism and the assumption that white people represent the entire community.
In practice, this leads to the creation of "handpicked communities." Instead of relying on a single identity, many Asian Americans build social relationships based on shared progressive politics. Thompson's case study on the tattooing culture imposed on Asian women reveals that Asian women have to contend with "racially coded stereotypes" (such as being labeled "good at math" or "uncreative") even in progressive or diverse workplaces. The application here is towards "solidarity within broader social movements" to address labor, environmental, and human rights issues, rather than accepting and being confined to certain identity frameworks.

Application of Concept #3: 
Risky subjectivity is particularly evident in Asian American communities affected by adoption, immigration, and war. Many Asian Americans grow up lacking knowledge of their cultural history or family past. This leads to gaps in identity. However, this risk can be a strength because by accepting that identity may be incomplete, Asian Americans can resist those stereotypes. Identity becomes more fluid rather than fixed. These concepts show that Asian American identity is shaped by power dynamics, but also by resistance and creativity.

Reflection of Concept #1:
These concepts relate to my life as an Asian American. I often limit my thoughts and behavior in public, especially when interacting with people who have higher social status. I realize that my appearance can influence how others treat me, which makes me even more cautious in my words and actions. After reading about surveillance, I understand that this feeling is not a weakness of mine, but something shaped by society.

Reflection of Concept #2:
I think that being subjected to racial discrimination is also related to my family’s experiences, because my parents often advised me to blend in and avoid causing trouble. They believed that being quiet and polite was the best and safest way to live. However, I realized that even when I accepted and followed these rules, I could still be misunderstood. People would often ask me where I was from or make assumptions based on my appearance and my behavior. This also made me realize that Asian American identity is often controlled by the expectations of others.

Reflection of Concept #3:
The concept of risk subjectivity has helped me better understand my own identity struggles. Like many other Asian Americans, I felt isolated from parts of my own culture and history. There was so much about my culture, history, and family’s past that I didn’t fully understand. Instead of viewing this as a problem, I now see it as a part of who I am. My identity doesn’t need to be complete or perfect because I can evolve and change over time.
